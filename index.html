How this works

A free, self-contained tool for building report-card comments. Open it from a web link or from a file on your computer — either way it runs entirely in your browser. Nothing you type is uploaded or stored online; your class list and comments stay with you.

⚠ Saving your work — please read this first

There is no autosave, and nothing is stored online or inside this page. Everything you do — your students and every edit to the comment bank — lives only in your browser until you save it to a file. Close the tab without saving and it is gone.

You have two ways to keep your work, both in the Save & load group at the top right. Each one saves everything — every subject's comments and your class list:

  1. Export data saves a small .json file to your Downloads. Keep it somewhere safe (a cloud drive or USB stick) — it is your only copy. Next time, click Import data and choose that file to pick up exactly where you left off.
  2. Download copy instead saves a fresh copy of this whole page with your work baked inside it. Re-open that downloaded file and everything is already there — no import step. (Browsers can't open the new file for you, so it's a quick manual hand-off.)

Use whichever you prefer; both are always available, and you can re-save whenever you've made changes worth keeping. Sharing a single subject with a colleague is a different thing with its own buttons — see Sharing one subject near the bottom.

Writing a comment

  1. Add your students on the left and choose how each should be referred to. To add a whole class at once, click Paste list and paste names one per line — a plain name, or columns copied straight from a spreadsheet (First, Last, Pronouns).
  2. Click a student, then add one or more Strengths and Growth areas. In a long class, the Filter students… box jumps to a name quickly.
  3. For each line, pick the level (1–4), the stem, and the evidence (what the student actually did). In any bank that lists assignments you can also name the assignment it came from. The sentence assembles itself underneath.
  4. Add an optional note for anything that doesn't fit the picker — a custom remark, an individual-plan goal, and so on. It is appended to the end of the comment.
  5. Click Copy comment and paste it wherever you enter report-card comments. The ‹ Prev / Next › buttons beside it step through your class without going back to the list, so you can copy, advance, copy, advance — and a small count (e.g. 7 / 30) shows where you are.

A small ✓/⚠ marker on each line shows when a piece is still missing. If a student has been started but a line is incomplete — a strength with no evidence, or a growth with no next step — a Needs attention list appears under the class list; click a name there to jump straight to that student.

Default picks (optional)

For any subject you can set what a brand-new Strength or Growth starts with, so you're not re-picking the same stem and evidence every time. On the Comment bank tab, expand Default picks for new strengths / growths and add up to three of each. The first line you add to a student uses #1, the second #2, the third #3; after that, new lines start blank. The level and proficiency word are still chosen per student.

Other comments

The Other comments section adds standalone statements that aren't tied to a particular strength or growth. Pick one of the built-in statements and tick the stem(s) it applies to — the wording switches between singular and plural automatically — or choose ✎ Write my own… to type a free-text comment for that student. You can reword, add, or remove these on the Comment bank tab; they are shared across all subjects.

Different subjects

The Bank menu at the top holds a separate comment bank for each subject — Science 9, English 9, Social 9, Math 9, CTF 8/9, English 6, Social 6, CTF 6 and Music 7-9 come ready to use. Switch between them with that menu, or click + New to start another subject and build its own stems and evidence. Each student keeps separate comments in each subject, and the active subject is shown beside their name. It all lives in the one file you save.

How each line is built

Every line is assembled from a few pieces so your comments stay consistent: a stem (the area being reported on), a proficiency word, and evidence of what the student did — and every Growth line ends with a next step. You choose the pieces; the tool handles the grammar, the a/an, and the punctuation.

Pronouns

The default is name only — the comment uses the student's name and avoids he/she/they. You can set she/her, he/him, or they/them per student, and the wording (verbs and possessives) adjusts automatically. Use whatever matches your school's practice for names and pronouns.

Editing your comment bank

Open the Comment bank tab to reword, add, or delete any option — proficiency words, stems, evidence, assignments, growth focuses, and next steps. This is where you tailor each subject to what you actually taught this term. A few extra controls live there too:

  • uses units — for subjects that organize work into topics or units (common in Science). When on, you can group evidence by unit and choose whether the unit name appears in the comment.
  • say “understanding” — makes lines read “demonstrates an excellent understanding in…” instead of “excellent proficiency in…” (the a/an is handled for you).
  • evidence varies by level — gives each piece of evidence four versions (4/3/2/1), so the wording itself shifts with the level rather than only the proficiency word.
  • Each assignment can be available on every stem, or limited to one (for example, an essay that only belongs under Writing).

When you write your own wording, these placeholders fill in per student:

  • {name} → the student's name
  • {sub} → he / she, or the name for they/them and name-only (keeps verbs correct)
  • {their} → his / her / their / the name's
  • {poss} → his / her / their, or "the" when no pronoun is set (avoids repeating the name)
  • {they} · {them} · {theyre} for he/she/they forms
  • {stems} → the stem name(s) ticked on an Other comment
  • {outcome|outcomes} → picks the first word for one stem, the second for several

It returns a ready-made bank file.

  1. Click Copy prompt below and paste it into your AI assistant.
  2. At the very bottom of the prompt, replace the bracketed line with your own past comments / outline / assessment list, then send it.
  3. Save the AI's reply as a file ending in .json (for example math8-bank.json).

HOW THE TOOL BUILDS A COMMENT
The teacher never types whole sentences. They pick small pieces and the tool joins them, so you are writing those pieces. There are two kinds of lines:

STRENGTH:
  "{name} demonstrates [WORD] proficiency [STEM-PREP] This was observed when {sub} [TASK]"
  Example: "Maria demonstrates strong proficiency in analyzing and solving problems through scientific reasoning. This was observed when she completed the long-answer section of her unit test."
    WORD      = a proficiency descriptor, e.g. "strong"
    STEM-PREP = "in analyzing and solving problems through scientific reasoning."
    TASK      = "completed the long-answer section of {their} unit test."

GROWTH:
  "{name} demonstrates [WORD] proficiency [STEM-PREP] As a next step, {sub} is encouraged [ACTION] [GOAL]"
  Example: "Maria demonstrates developing proficiency in developing skills for inquiry and communication. As a next step, she is encouraged to plan investigations more carefully to reach clearer evidence-based conclusions."
    ACTION = "to plan investigations more carefully"        (NO ending period)
    GOAL   = "to reach clearer evidence-based conclusions."  (ends with a period)

So every line opens the same way ("{name} demonstrates ___ proficiency" + the stem's prep phrase), then adds evidence (strength) or a next step (growth).

WRITING RULES (these are what keep the assembled sentences grammatical)
1. Write every TASK, ACTION and GOAL so it continues from a SINGULAR third-person subject (he / she / the student's name). Use singular verbs: "completed", "used", "solves", "demonstrates" -- never "complete" or "completing".
   TASK   follows "...when {sub} "       e.g. "completed a lab on chemical reactions."
   ACTION follows "...is encouraged "     e.g. "to revise written work before submitting"
   GOAL   follows the action              e.g. "to communicate ideas more clearly."
2. STEM-PREP completes "demonstrates ___ proficiency ___". Begin it with a preposition (in / when / while / with) and END IT WITH A PERIOD, e.g. "in understanding key concepts."
3. Periods: STEM-PREP, TASK and GOAL each END WITH A PERIOD. ACTION has NO period (a goal is added after it); if an action has no goal, then give it a period. Descriptor words carry NO punctuation.
4. Placeholders you may use inside any TASK / ACTION / GOAL (the tool fills them in per student): {their} = his/her/their/the name's; {sub} = he/she/the name; {poss} = his/her/their, or "the". Use {their} for possessives ("completed {their} test."); never write "his/her".
5. Use Canadian spelling (organize, behaviour, colour, analyze, centre).
6. Keep everything the teacher gives you; invent only what is needed to fill gaps, and keep all wording professional and report-card appropriate.

FIELD NOTES
- "subject": the bank's name in the Bank menu; keep it short, like "Subject Grade" (e.g. "Math 8").
- "stems": the 3 to 6 reporting strands of the subject (e.g. Reading, Writing; or Understanding, Reasoning, Inquiry). Each needs a unique short "id" and a "prep" phrase (rule 2).
- "descriptors": proficiency words for level 4 (highest) down to 1 (lowest). Keep or tailor the sample words; each level needs at least one.
- "headNoun": keep "proficiency" unless you prefer lines like "demonstrates a strong understanding in..." -- then use "understanding".
- "tasks": the STRENGTH evidence -- things a student did that show the strength. Tie each to a stem with "stemId". Aim for several per stem; each ends with a period (rule 3).
- "actions" / "nextSteps": the GROWTH pieces. action = what to work on (no period); nextStep = the goal it builds toward (period). Set "stemId":"" to offer a piece on every stem, or a stem id to limit it to that stem.
- "assignments" (optional): named pieces of work, e.g. { "id": "ess", "text": "the In-Class Essay", "stemId": "" }. If set, a strength can be tied to one and reads "This was observed in the In-Class Essay, where {sub} ...". Most banks can leave this [].
- "units" (optional, mainly for Science): set "usesUnits": true and list { "id": "u1", "label": "Chemistry", "context": "our chemistry unit" }, then give each task a matching "unitId"; the line then reads "This was observed during our chemistry unit when {sub} ...". If unsure, leave "usesUnits": false and "units": [].
- IDs may be any short unique strings; every "stemId" and "unitId" must match an id you defined above.

Sharing one subject with a colleague

Sharing a single subject's bank is separate from saving your own work. It has its own buttons on the Comment bank tab — not the Save & load group at the top, which always handles your whole file (every subject plus your class list).

To send a colleague one subject, without your class list or your other subjects: open the Comment bank tab, switch to that subject, and click Share this bank — it downloads a small bank file. Your colleague opens their own copy, goes to the Comment bank tab, and clicks Add a shared bank to load it. It arrives as a new subject in their Bank menu; if they already have a subject with that name, they're asked whether to replace it or keep both.

Sharing the whole tool

To give someone the entire tool, send them this HTML file, or the web link if it's hosted — no accounts, no sign-in. They can start using it immediately and save their own work the same way you do.
